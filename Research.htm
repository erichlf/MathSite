Current Research

My research interests are centered around the development of mathematically justified models and corresponding efficient, accurate algorithms for grand challenge problems at the frontier of computational science and engineering. My current interest is in the application of these ideas to climate modeling, such as the Quasi-Geostrophic Flow Equations (QGE), flow in porous media with an emphasis on density dependent flow and contaminate transport. In both areas I emphasize the use of finite elements (FE), including error analysis and efficient algorithms such as the two-level method.


Wave Generation:
The problem of wave generation is complex and has many applications-- one such application is in the creation of artificial waves for surfing. Traditionally wave generators have used either paddles, on laboratory scale, or a mass of water, on larger scales, to generate waves. However, we consider a relatively new method of wave generation, that is, we consider an underwater moving object which displaces the water to create a wave. This idea of wave generation is similar to the way a tsunami is created, in which the wave is created by either an underwater landslide or earthquake [9]. This type of artificial wave generator has been recently developed at Wave Garden© located in the Basque Country - Spain [8]. The design of such wave generators is generally done by trial and error. In collaboration with Enrique Zuazua, Denys Dutykh, and Robin Goix; our goal is to extend the work done by Nersisyan et al [10], where they considered the optimal shape design of such wave generators, from the 1-D case into the 2-D case.

Figure 1: Ocean cross-section with moving object \(\zeta\).
To this end, we consider \((x,y,z)\) a Cartesian coordinate system, with \(z\) measuring upwards from the average water level-- letting \(\eta(x,y,t)\) be the height of the free surface at a time \(t\), and \(h(x,y,t) = D(x,y) + \zeta(x,y,t)\) the seabed profile, which is assumed to be the sum of a time-independent part \(D\), the seabed elevation, and a time-dependant part \(\zeta\), the underwater moving object (see Figure 2).

With this description of the wave generator in place the following Boussinesq system can be derived from the Euler equations [9,12]
\begin{equation} \begin{split} \mathbf{u}_t + \nabla \eta + \epsilon (\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla) \mathbf{u} &- \sigma^2\frac{h}{2}\nabla (\nabla \cdot (h \mathbf{u}_t)) + \sigma^2 \frac{h^2}{6}\nabla (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u}_t) - \sigma^2\frac{h}{2}\nabla \zeta_{tt} = \mathcal{O}(\epsilon \sigma^2, \sigma^4) \\ &\qquad\eta_t+\zeta_t + \nabla \cdot [(h+\epsilon\eta)\mathbf{u}] = 0 \end{split} \label{eqn:Peregrine} \end{equation}
While this model can be discretized in a pretty straight forward manner, using Taylor-Hood finite elements, the time required to resolve all scales, when realistic parameter values are used, is unreasonable when considering the wave object will need to be optimized. By not resolving all scales one can save time, however stability is lost and the solution is not accurate (see Figure 2). Thus, numerical methods which improve computational time while not sacrificing accuracy are crucial to be able to optimize the wave object. To this end, we plan on using mesh adaptive stabilization to improve computational time, efficiency, and stability. As can be seen the initial results for the stabilized \(P^1-P^1\) FE (\autoref{sfig:Stable}), without adaptivity, clearly results in a solution which appears to be more stable, but the size of the wave appears to be smaller than the solution given by the fine mesh (\autoref{sfig:Fine}). However, the solution for the stabilized \(P^1-P^1\) FE took 50% less time as compared to the Taylor-Hood FE on the same mesh.

Climate Modeling:
Figure 3: Subpolar and Subtropical Gyres of the Mid-Atlantic The Quasi-Geostrophic equations (QGE) are simplified models of large scale oceanic or atmospheric flows and are often used in climate modeling [3]. The QGE describe a double gyre system that are characteristic of mid-latitude oceans [3,8,11] (See Figure 1). For the QGE, we consider the pure streamfunction form
\begin{equation} -\Delta \psi_t + Re^{-1} \Delta^2 \psi + J(\psi, \Delta \psi) - Ro^{-1} \psi_x = Ro^{-1} F, \label{eqn:QGE} \end{equation} where \(\psi\), \(Re\), and \(Ro\) are the streamfunction, the Reynolds number, and Rossby number, respectively, while \[ J(\psi,\Delta \psi) = \psi_x\, \Delta \psi_y - \psi_y\, \Delta \psi_x \] is the Jacobian and \(F\) is the external forcing. To completely specify the system we consider the boundary conditions \(\psi = \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial \mathbf{n}} = 0\). In [5] we applied a finite element (FE) discretization to the QGE. This is the first time a \(C^1\) FE has been used to discretize the QGE. In [5] we presented rigorous error analysis and numerical experiments.

Most FE discretizations of the QGE are for the streamfunction-voriticity formulation of the QGE, and the reason is simple. The pure streamfunction form is a fourth order PDE, requiring \(C^1\) FEs for conforming FEs, such as the Argyris element. However, the advantage of the pure streamfunction formulation is the reduction of two variables to a single variable. In addition to algorithm development, I have proven in [5], for the first time, optimal error estimates for the FE discretizations of the QGE.

Theorem Let \(\psi\) be the solution to the stationary QGE and \(\psi^h\) be the solution of the FE formulation of stationary QGE. Assume that the small data condition is satisfied: \begin{equation} Re^{-2} \, Ro \geq \Gamma_1 \, \| F \|_{-2} . \label{eqn:small_data_condition_dual} \end{equation} Furthermore, assume that \(\psi\in H^6(\Omega) \cap H^2_0(\Omega)\). Then there exist positive constants \(C_0, \, C_1 \text{ and } C_2\) that can depend on \(Re\), \(Ro\), \(\Gamma_1\), \(F\), but not on the mesh size \(h\), such that \begin{align} |\psi - \psi^h|_2 &\le C_2 \, h^4 , \label{eqn:H2Error} \\ |\psi - \psi^h|_1 &\le C_1 \, h^5 , \label{eqn:H1Error} \\ \|\psi - \psi^h\|_0 &\le C_0 \, h^6 . \label{eqn:L2Error} \end{align}
While in [7] I proved a similar result for the time-dependent QGE.
